Part 1 — street_name Frequency Encoding

The problem: 500+ street names confuse the model

Our dataset has over 500 unique street names: Ang Mo Kio Ave 3, Buona Vista Rd, Holland Rd, Jurong West St 42...

Tree-based models like XGBoost can handle categories, but 500 levels is a lot of noise. Some streets appear only 5 times — the model can barely learn anything from them.

The fix: count how often each street appears

Instead of passing the raw street name, we replace it with how many transactions happened on that street in the training data.

street_name          → street_freq
--------------------------------------
ANG MO KIO AVE 3     → 3,842   (very popular street, many transactions)
HOLLAND RD           → 412     (premium area, moderate volume)
BUONA VISTA RD       → 89      (smaller estate)
COMPASSVALE BOW      → 12      (newer, fewer transactions)

Analogy: Imagine you're trying to predict restaurant bill size. Instead of knowing the restaurant's full name, you know "this restaurant has served 5,000 customers" vs "this one has served 20." A busy restaurant is likely in a premium location with higher bills. Same logic applies to HDB streets — high-frequency streets tend to be in well-established estates with more consistent pricing.

Why is this safe? (No data leakage)

Some features can "cheat" by using information that includes price data (called data leakage). street_freq is safe because it only counts how many transactions happened — it never looks at the price. It's purely a volume signal.

# Compute from training set only
STREET_FREQ = train['street_name'].value_counts().to_dict()

# Apply to both train and test
df['street_freq'] = df['street_name'].map(STREET_FREQ).fillna(0)
# fillna(0) = streets in test that never appeared in training get count = 0

Part 2 — What is Stacking?

The problem with blending (v5, v6)

In v5 and v6, we combined XGBoost and LightGBM predictions using a fixed ratio:

v6 blend: 57% × XGB prediction + 43% × LGBM prediction = final prediction

This fixed ratio applies the same weight to every single flat — whether it's a 3-room flat in Jurong, a 5-room flat in Bishan, or an Executive flat in Queenstown. But what if XGBoost is better at predicting mature estate prices, and LightGBM is better at newer estates?

Analogy — Fixed blend is like a recipe that never changes:
Imagine two chefs (XGBoost and LightGBM). Chef A is brilliant at cooking Asian food. Chef B excels at Western food. Fixed blending says: "Always serve 57% of Chef A's dish + 43% of Chef B's dish regardless of what the customer ordered." Stacking says: "When the customer orders Asian food, trust Chef A more. When they order Western, trust Chef B more."

Stacking — a smarter way to combine models

Stacking uses a third model (the meta-model) to learn the optimal combination. Here's the full 3-step process:

Step 1: Generate Out-of-Fold (OOF) predictions

We split training data into 5 folds. For each fold, we train XGB and LGBM on the other 4 folds and predict the held-out fold:

Fold 1 (20K rows):  Train on folds 2+3+4+5 → XGB predicts fold 1 → xgb_oof[fold1]
Fold 2 (20K rows):  Train on folds 1+3+4+5 → XGB predicts fold 2 → xgb_oof[fold2]
Fold 3 (20K rows):  Train on folds 1+2+4+5 → XGB predicts fold 3 → xgb_oof[fold3]
Fold 4 (20K rows):  Train on folds 1+2+3+5 → XGB predicts fold 4 → xgb_oof[fold4]
Fold 5 (20K rows):  Train on folds 1+2+3+4 → XGB predicts fold 5 → xgb_oof[fold5]

After all 5 folds, every training row has an OOF prediction. The same is done for LightGBM. Result: a table with 2 columns:

Row    | xgb_oof_prediction | lgbm_oof_prediction | actual_price
-------|-------------------|---------------------|-------------
Row 1  | log(623,000)      | log(618,000)        | log(625,000)
Row 2  | log(412,000)      | log(415,000)        | log(410,000)
Row 3  | log(870,000)      | log(855,000)        | log(880,000)
...150,633 rows total...

Why out-of-fold? If we trained XGBoost on all training data and then asked it to predict those same rows, the predictions would be "too good" — the model memorised those rows during training. OOF forces each row to be predicted by a model that has never seen it before, giving honest predictions.

Step 2: Train the Ridge meta-model

Ridge Regression is a simple linear model. We train it on the 2-column OOF table:

meta_X_train = [[xgb_oof[0], lgbm_oof[0]],    # row 0: XGB says this, LGBM says that
                [xgb_oof[1], lgbm_oof[1]],    # row 1
                ...150,633 rows...]

ridge.fit(meta_X_train, y_log)   # Ridge learns: given both predictions, what's the best answer?

Ridge learned these coefficients:

final_log_price = 0.5704 × xgb_prediction + 0.4305 × lgbm_prediction + (–0.012)
                  ↑ trust XGB 57%           ↑ trust LGBM 43%           ↑ small bias correction

Step 3: Predict test data

For test rows, we can't generate true OOF predictions (test has no price labels). So we average the test predictions from all 5 fold models:

xgb_test_avg  = average of 5 fold XGB models predicting test rows
lgbm_test_avg = average of 5 fold LGBM models predicting test rows

final_pred = expm1( Ridge.predict([[xgb_test_avg, lgbm_test_avg]]) )

Part 3 — Why Ridge and Not Something More Complex?

Our meta-model has only 2 input features (XGB prediction and LGBM prediction). With so few inputs, a complex model would massively overfit.

Analogy: Imagine you have 2 weather forecasters — one for rain, one for temperature. You want to combine their forecasts. You could hire a junior meteorologist (Ridge — simple, reliable) or a full research team (complex neural network). With only 2 inputs, the research team would start reading tea leaves — far too complex for the job. The junior meteorologist does it fine.

Why Ridge specifically? (not plain Linear Regression)

Ridge adds a regularisation penalty — it discourages the model from assigning extreme weights. This prevents the coefficients from blowing up when XGB and LGBM predictions are highly correlated (which they are).

Plain Linear Regression might learn:  2.3 × XGB – 1.8 × LGBM  ← unstable, extreme weights
Ridge (alpha=0.001) learned:          0.57 × XGB + 0.43 × LGBM ← stable, sensible weights

Alpha sweep — finding the right regularisation strength

We tested 6 values of alpha (the regularisation strength). Lower alpha = less penalty = more freedom:

Part 5 — The CV–Kaggle Gap: Why It Collapsed

One of the most impressive results of v7 was the CV–Kaggle gap shrinking from $306 → $64:

VersionOOF / CV RMSEKaggle RMSEGapWhat it means
v5$21,570$22,428$858Large gap — target leakage in CV
v6$21,818$22,124$306Smaller gap — OOF encoding helped
v7$21,841$21,906$64Tiny gap — model generalises very well

What does the gap tell us?
CV RMSE is measured on training data (where the model has "seen the neighbourhood"). Kaggle RMSE is on truly unseen test data. A small gap means the model performs almost as well on new data as on training data — this is the ideal.

A large gap (like v5's $858) usually signals overfitting or leakage — the model was unknowingly given hints during training that it couldn't use on test data.

Why did v7's gap collapse to $64? Three reasons working together:

  1. OOF encoding (from v6) — town_median_price no longer leaks target information
  2. OOF stacking — the meta-model was also built without seeing the test set
  3. Averaging test predictions across 5 folds — reduces variance in the final test predictions

Part 7 — Why We Train on 100% Data for the Final Submission

During development, we split data 80/20 to tune hyperparameters. But for the Kaggle submission, we retrained on all 150,633 rows.

Analogy: During exam preparation, you study from past papers (80%) and test yourself on mock questions (20%) to check your understanding. But on exam day, you bring everything you studied — all the past papers. You don't throw away 20% of your notes just because you used them for mock tests.

Why keep both raw and engineered versions of some signals?
For example, we keep raw Latitude/Longitude AND engineered dist_to_cbd. Latitude and Longitude let the model learn fine-grained geographic patterns (e.g. specific neighbourhoods). dist_to_cbd gives it an explicit "how far from city centre" number. Both together give the model more ways to capture location effects.

What Could We Try Next?

  • CatBoost as third base model — handles categorical features (town, flat_type, street_name) natively without OrdinalEncoder. Stack becomes Ridge on [XGB, LGBM, CAT] OOF — 3 independent signals for Ridge to combine.
  • Optuna hyperparameter tuning — Bayesian optimisation that learns from previous trials (smarter than random search). 100 Optuna trials ≈ 300 random trials in quality.
  • block_num encoding — extract the numeric part of block number (e.g. "153A" → 153). Corner blocks or low-numbered landmark blocks sometimes carry premiums.
